Structure type: Mosque
Built by: Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan
Architechtural style: Islamic
Construction period: 1644 - 1656
Location: Chandni Chowk

Masjid-i Jehan Numa is commonly known as the Jama Masjid (friday mosque) of Delhi. It was built by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan at a cost of one million rupees, and was inaugurated by Imam Syed Abdul Ghafoor Shah Bukhari in 1656. Being one of the last monuments built under Shah Jahan, it remained the royal mosque of the emperors until the end of the Mughal period.

The highly decorative mosque has three great gates, four towers and two 40 m-high minarets constructed of strips of red sandstone and white marble. The minarets consist of five storeys, each with a protruding balcony. An open courtyard facing the eastern gateway of the mosque building can accommodate 25,000 people. The largest interior space is the prayer hall, where there are calligraphic inscriptions in Persian above whose entrances and three large marble domes rise from the roof.

There is a collection of relics of Muhammad – the Quran written on deerskin, a red beard-hair of the prophet, his sandals and his footprints embedded in a marble block.
